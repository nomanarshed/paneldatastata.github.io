Panel data is everywhere in applied econometrics. Firms tracked over time, countries across decades, individuals in longitudinal surveys — if your data has both a cross-sectional and a time dimension, you are in panel territory. Stata has deep support for it. The challenge is that the methods scatter across a wide range: fixed effects, GMM, ARDL, quantile regression, cointegration, structural breaks.

This playlist organises 55 videos into a sensible learning path. Work through the stages in order. Each one assumes the previous. The methods build on each other in ways that are not always obvious from the title of a single video.

Stage 1

Data Preparation

Every analysis is only as reliable as the data behind it. Before a single model runs, you need your panel structured correctly — declared to Stata, free of gaps, clean of problem rows, and consistent across sources. This stage covers the full preparation workflow, including some often-overlooked tasks: handling repeated time values, constructing composite indices, and diagnosing missing data patterns. It is unglamorous work, but skipping it is where most analyses silently go wrong.

A note on sequencing

Stages 1 and 2 are prerequisites, not suggestions. If your panel is not declared correctly, commands like xtreg and xtdpd will either fail or silently produce wrong results. If you do not understand what fixed effects are doing, the logic of GMM instruments will not make sense when you reach Stage 3.

Stages 3 and 4 have more flexibility depending on your research focus. If you are working on cointegration, you can pull those Stage 4 videos in after finishing Stage 3. If your interest is distributional analysis, the quantile videos can run alongside the Stage 3 estimators. The more foundation you have before specialising, the better your instincts will be when something goes wrong — and in panel econometrics, something usually does.

The course is long. That seems right. These methods are genuinely hard to use well, and the places where they tend to break are not always obvious until a reviewer asks a question you should have been asking yourself.
